# Provocation Lenses

These are the angles from which provocations are generated. Each lens looks at the
student's work from a different direction — the way a good teacher would naturally
ask different kinds of questions. Not all lenses apply to every discipline or every
piece of work. Select lenses based on the discipline, the specific work, and the
detected weaknesses.

When presenting provocations to the student, use the plain-language lens name (the
one below). Do not use technical or academic-sounding labels. The student should
understand what kind of challenge they are facing without needing a glossary.

## Universal Lenses (apply across all disciplines)

**The Other Side**
What would someone arguing the opposite position say? Where would they attack first?
Targets: The main claim, the weakest supporting reason, logical inferences.

**Hidden Assumptions**
What unstated assumptions does this analysis depend on? Which are verifiable, which are
contested, which are invisible to the author?
Targets: Premises the student treats as given but that are actually debatable.

**Show Your Evidence**
For each factual claim, where is the evidence? Is the evidence sufficient for the strength
of the claim being made?
Targets: Unsupported claims, strong claims on weak evidence, evidence that supports a
weaker version of the claim than stated.

**Explain It Simply**
Can you explain this to someone outside your discipline who needs to act on it?
Targets: Jargon, abstraction, conclusions that are technically correct but operationally
meaningless.

**Where Does This Break?**
Under what conditions does this analysis break down? Where are its boundaries?
Targets: Overgeneralisation, failure to specify scope, unexamined edge cases.

**What Could Change?**
What could change in the near future that would invalidate this analysis?
Targets: Analyses that treat a current state of affairs as permanent.

## Discipline-Adaptive Lenses

These lenses activate based on the identified discipline. Select and phrase them
appropriately for the field.

**Is This the Right Method?** (sciences, social sciences, empirical research)
Is the method appropriate for the question? Would a different method produce a different
answer?

**Fact vs. Opinion** (philosophy, ethics, policy, law)
Are you describing what is, or arguing for what should be? Where does the analysis slide
between these without flagging the transition?

**Does This Apply Here?** (law, regulation, policy, comparative studies)
Does this analysis account for the specific jurisdiction, context, or system it applies
to? Where might it not transfer?

**What Is Your Source?** (law, medicine, engineering, any field with authoritative sources)
What authority supports this position? Is the authority current, binding, and applied
within its proper scope?

**A Different Lens** (any field with multiple theoretical approaches)
Your analysis uses [framework X]. If you applied [framework Y] to the same problem, would
you reach the same conclusion?

**What Did You Give Up?** (engineering, architecture, product design, system design)
Every design choice has a cost. What did you give up by choosing this approach?
