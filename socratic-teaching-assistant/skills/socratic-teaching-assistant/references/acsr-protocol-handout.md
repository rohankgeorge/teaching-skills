# The ACSR Protocol: Using AI as a Thinking Partner

A structured method for engaging with AI that deepens analytical reasoning instead of
bypassing it.

---

## The Problem

When students use AI in its default mode, the AI does the thinking and the student does
the accepting. The output may look competent, but the student's own analytical skills have
not been exercised. Over time, this pattern weakens the very capabilities the education is
meant to build.

## The Principle

AI should increase the cognitive demand on the user, not decrease it. The ACSR protocol
restructures the interaction so that AI acts as a rigorous thinking partner — one that
challenges, questions, and provokes rather than drafts, completes, and polishes.

## The Four Phases

### Phase 1: ARTICULATE

Before engaging with AI, the student commits to a position. There are two ways this
happens, depending on what the student brings.

**If the student brings their own argument or analysis,** they write out:

- Their main claim or conclusion
- The key reasons or evidence supporting it
- Any assumptions they are aware of making
- The part of their argument they feel least confident about

**If the student brings a document they need to understand** (a research paper, a case,
a chapter, an assigned reading), they write out:

- What they think the author is arguing — in their own words
- What evidence or reasoning the author uses
- What assumptions the author appears to make
- What part of the document they find most difficult or unconvincing

In the document-comprehension path, the AI does not explain the source material. The
student must attempt their own reading first. If they say "I don't understand it," the
AI narrows the scope ("Which part? Read just the first section and tell me what you think
it says, even if you're guessing") rather than providing a summary.

The purpose in both cases is pre-commitment. A student who has stated a position — or
committed to a reading — has something to defend. This transforms the AI's subsequent
challenges from abstract exercises into personal ones.

Without this phase, the rest of the protocol collapses into passive consumption.

### Phase 2: CHALLENGE

The AI reads the student's articulated position (or their reading of the document) and
generates targeted provocations. These are not suggestions for improvement. They are
questions and counterarguments that stress-test the reasoning.

For own-argument sessions, provocations challenge the student's claims, evidence, and
assumptions. For document-comprehension sessions, provocations test the accuracy and
depth of the student's reading against the actual text — pointing to specific parts of
the document the student missed, misread, or glossed over, and asking them to re-examine.

Each provocation:
- Names the specific element of the argument it targets
- Identifies what type of challenge it represents (questioning evidence, testing scope,
  surfacing an assumption, introducing a competing perspective, testing reading accuracy)
- Is phrased as a genuine intellectual challenge, not a rhetorical trap

The AI does not provide answers, alternative arguments, or hints about what the "right"
position is. In document-comprehension sessions, it does not explain what the document
says — it asks questions that guide the student toward a more accurate reading.

### Phase 3: STRENGTHEN

The student responds to each provocation. For each challenge, they:
- Accept the challenge and modify their position (explaining what changed and why), or
- Rebut the challenge (explaining why their original position survives it), or
- Incorporate the challenge as a qualification or limitation

If the student ignores a provocation, the AI flags it and asks whether the provocation
was unpersuasive or simply difficult. Both are legitimate, but the distinction matters
for the quality of the analysis.

The AI does not generate revisions. The student does all the writing.

### Phase 4: REFLECT

The AI generates a structured map of what changed between the original and revised
positions — not a quality judgment, but a record of the analytical journey:

- Claims that were modified, qualified, or abandoned
- Assumptions that were made explicit
- Evidence that was added or re-evaluated
- Perspectives that were incorporated
- Gaps that remain unaddressed

The student then answers three reflection questions:
1. Which challenge was most difficult to respond to, and why?
2. What did you discover about your own reasoning that you were not aware of before?
3. If you were to start this analysis again from scratch, what would you do differently?

The full interaction — original position, provocations, responses, reflections, and
revised position — is compiled into a Thinking Journey document. This document is the
submittable artefact. It demonstrates not just the student's final answer, but the quality
of their engagement with the thinking process.

## Calibration: The Tool Adapts to the Student

The Socratic Teaching Assistant calibrates itself along two independent axes.

**Language level** determines how provocations are phrased. The AI assesses the student's
English fluency from their first substantial input and adjusts its register to match. A
student with foundational fluency receives one clear question at a time in plain language.
A student with advanced fluency receives dense, multi-layered provocations that name
specific reasoning structures. The goal is that cognitive effort goes toward analytical
thinking, not toward decoding vocabulary.

**Provocation intensity** determines how challenging the provocations are. This is separate
from language level — a student with basic English may still be a sophisticated thinker who
needs strong challenges, just phrased simply. Four levels range from Introductory (2-3
fundamental provocations with generous scaffolding) to Adversarial (5+ provocations with
iterative follow-ups, targeting reasoning methodology as well as specific arguments).

Both axes recalibrate silently during the session if the student's responses suggest
miscalibration. Over multiple sessions, the tool tracks the student's growth and adjusts
calibration based on demonstrated improvement.

---

## Across Sessions: The Self-Learning System

When used repeatedly with the same student, the tool maintains a learner profile that
tracks reasoning patterns, strengths, weaknesses, and growth over time.

After each session, the tool records observations: which provocations the student engaged
with substantively, which were dismissed, what reasoning patterns recurred, and what
changed compared to prior sessions. At the start of the next session, the tool reads the
profile and calibrates accordingly.

This means the tool gets better at challenging each individual student over time. It
learns which lenses target their specific blind spots, when they are ready for increased
intensity, and which strengths it can build on.

The learner profile is also a valuable artefact for instructors: it provides a longitudinal
view of a student's critical thinking development, not just a snapshot from a single
assignment.

## For Instructors: Using the Protocol in Class

The ACSR protocol can be used in several ways:

**As an individual assignment.** Students complete all four phases independently, using
AI in student mode. They submit the Thinking Journey document. Assessment focuses on
the quality of engagement (how substantively did the student respond to challenges?)
rather than the quality of the final position alone.

**As a classroom exercise.** The instructor uses AI in instructor mode to generate
provocations against a sample analysis (student-submitted or synthetic). The instructor
selects which provocations to surface and facilitates class discussion around them. Students
experience the Challenge and Strengthen phases collectively.

**As an assessment design tool.** The instructor uses the provocation lenses to design exam
or assignment questions that test the same analytical skills the protocol develops —
assumption identification, counterargument response, scope limitation, evidence evaluation.

**As a peer review framework.** Students use the provocation lenses (without AI) to
challenge each other's work. The lenses provide a structured vocabulary for giving
analytical feedback rather than surface-level editing suggestions.

## The Provocation Lenses

Provocations are generated through analytical lenses — structured perspectives that
target different aspects of reasoning. Not all lenses apply to every discipline or
every piece of work.

**The Other Side:** What would someone arguing the opposite position say? Where would
they attack first?

**Hidden Assumptions:** What unstated assumptions does this analysis depend on? Which
are verifiable, which are contested?

**Show Your Evidence:** For each factual claim, where is the evidence? Does the evidence
support the specific claim made, or only a weaker version of it?

**Explain It Simply:** Can you explain this to a non-specialist who needs to act on it?
What decision does this analysis actually support?

**Where Does This Break?** Under what conditions does this analysis break down? Where
are its boundaries?

**What Could Change?** What could change in the near future that would invalidate this
analysis?

Additional lenses activate based on the discipline — "Is This the Right Method?" for
empirical work, "Fact vs. Opinion" for ethics and policy, "Does This Apply Here?" for
law and regulation, "A Different Lens" for theory-dependent fields.

## Theoretical Foundations

This protocol draws on established research in learning science and human-AI interaction:

- Cognitive forcing functions (Buçinca et al. 2021): Interventions that require the user
  to engage before seeing AI output, reducing passive acceptance.
- Desirable difficulty (Bjork 1994): Productive cognitive struggle improves learning
  outcomes even when it slows immediate performance.
- AI as tool for thought vs. AI as assistant (Sarkar 2023-2025): The distinction between
  AI that optimises for task completion and AI that optimises for the quality of human
  cognition.
- Provocations (Drosos, Sarkar et al. 2025): Short textual interventions that critique AI
  suggestions and propose alternatives, empirically shown to induce critical thinking at
  every level of Bloom's taxonomy.
- Double-loop learning (Argyris 1977): The outer loop questions the goals and frameworks
  that produced the errors, not just the errors themselves.
