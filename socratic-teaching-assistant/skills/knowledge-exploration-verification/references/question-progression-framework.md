# Question Progression Framework: Detailed Examples

This reference provides multiple example questions for each of the seven stages across
different disciplines. Use these examples to understand the cognitive demand of each stage
and to generate questions tailored to your student's specific source material and context.

---

## Stage 1: Foundational Factual

### Purpose Reminder
Tests whether the student knows the essential facts that all subsequent reasoning depends on.
Not trivia — core facts necessary for understanding.

### Law: Constitutional Cases

**Source:** Marbury v. Madison (1803) — jurisdiction of Supreme Court

**Example questions:**
- "What was the central legal question before the Court? What did the Court decide?"
- "Marbury held what office? Madison was what? What prevented Marbury from getting his commission?"
- "What constitutional provision was at issue? What did the Court conclude about it?"
- "Which branch of government was the court concerned with? What power did it claim?"

### Medicine: Clinical Trial

**Source:** A published randomized controlled trial of a new treatment

**Example questions:**
- "What condition was the study investigating? What was the proposed treatment?"
- "How many participants did the study include? What were the inclusion criteria?"
- "What was the primary outcome the study measured?"
- "Did the treatment work? What did the results show?"

### Philosophy: Kant's Categorical Imperative

**Source:** Kant's Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals, relevant passages

**Example questions:**
- "What is Kant's central claim about how we should act?"
- "What is a 'categorical imperative,' according to Kant? How does it differ from other kinds of commands?"
- "What example does Kant use to illustrate the categorical imperative?"
- "According to Kant, what makes an action moral or immoral?"

### Economics: Comparative Advantage Theory

**Source:** Ricardo's Principles of Political Economy, or a modern textbook treatment

**Example questions:**
- "What is comparative advantage? How does it differ from absolute advantage?"
- "What does Ricardo claim about specialization and trade?"
- "What is the core example Ricardo uses — what countries and what goods?"
- "According to the theory, who benefits from trade?"

### History: The Fall of the Berlin Wall

**Source:** A historical account (book chapter, academic article, or primary documents)

**Example questions:**
- "What year did the Berlin Wall fall? What events led to it?"
- "Who decided to open the wall? Under what pressures?"
- "What were the immediate consequences?"
- "What political systems were divided by the wall?"

### Science: Evolution by Natural Selection

**Source:** Darwin's Origin of Species or a modern biology textbook treatment

**Example questions:**
- "What variation does Darwin observe in populations? What happens to that variation?"
- "What claim does Darwin make about inheritance?"
- "How does Darwin explain the origin of new species?"
- "What is the evidence Darwin cites for his theory?"

## Stage 2: Interpretation

### Purpose Reminder
Test whether the student can paraphrase and accurately restate the literal content without inference.
Interpretation is comprehension — understanding what the author says, not evaluating it.

### Law: A Court's Reasoning

**Source:** A judicial opinion establishing a legal principle (e.g., Miranda v. Arizona)

**Example questions:**
- "In your own words, what is the Court saying about what police must do? Don't evaluate it — just explain what the ruling requires."
- "How does the Court describe the problem it is addressing? What is wrong, in the Court's view?"
- "The Court quotes this passage: [specific quote]. What is the Court claiming that passage shows?"
- "Restate the Court's main reasoning in one or two sentences. What is the core argument?"

### Medicine: A Study's Findings

**Source:** A research paper's Results and Discussion sections

**Example questions:**
- "What did the study find? State the main result in plain language."
- "What does the paper say the result means? (Not what you think it means — what does the paper say?)"
- "The paper mentions 'statistical significance.' How does the paper use that term? What does it mean in this context?"
- "How does the paper describe the limitations of the study? What does it say might be uncertain?"

### Philosophy: An Argument's Claim

**Source:** An excerpt from a philosophical text making a substantive claim

**Example questions:**
- "What is the author claiming here? Restate it without evaluating it."
- "The author uses the term [X] in this passage. Based on context, what does the author mean by [X]?"
- "What reasons does the author give for this claim? List them."
- "In your own words, what is the author arguing?"

### Economics: A Theoretical Claim

**Source:** A textbook or paper explaining an economic principle

**Example questions:**
- "According to the source, how do prices respond to scarcity? What does the source say happens?"
- "What does the source claim about the relationship between supply and demand?"
- "Restate the source's explanation of [economic phenomenon] in your own words."
- "What mechanism does the source describe? Walk through it step by step."

### History: A Primary Source Account

**Source:** A historical document (letter, memoir, official record, contemporary account)

**Example questions:**
- "What is the author of this document describing? What happened, according to them?"
- "What is the author's perspective or position? Who were they?"
- "What does the source claim about [specific event]?"
- "In plain language, what is this document saying?"

### Science: A Study's Methods

**Source:** The Methods section of a research paper

**Example questions:**
- "What did the researchers do? Describe their procedure."
- "What was the study design? (Was it a laboratory experiment, a survey, an observational study, etc.?)"
- "What measurements did the researchers take? What were they measuring?"
- "Why did the researchers choose this approach? What does the source say about their rationale?"

## Stage 3: Structural

### Purpose Reminder
Test whether the student sees the architecture of the argument — why claims are presented in a
particular sequence and how different parts fit together.

### Law: A Court Opinion's Organization

**Source:** A multisection judicial opinion

**Example questions:**
- "The Court first establishes [principle A]. Only then does it discuss [principle B]. Why this order? What role does A play in understanding B?"
- "How is this opinion organized? What comes first, and what builds on it?"
- "Why does the Court include [historical background section]? What does that background establish before the Court's main argument?"
- "The Court spends considerable space on [issue X], then briefly addresses [issue Y]. Why the different emphasis? What is the relationship?"

### Medicine: A Paper's Structure

**Source:** A complete research paper

**Example questions:**
- "Why does the paper include a Literature Review? How does it connect to the Methods section that follows?"
- "The Discussion section raises [concern A] that the Methods section didn't explicitly address. Why does the author raise it in Discussion?"
- "How does the paper's Introduction set up the hypothesis? What path does it take to get there?"
- "Why present the Results before the Discussion? What does that sequence accomplish?"

### Philosophy: An Argument's Architecture

**Source:** A philosophical argument (e.g., a series of premises leading to a conclusion)

**Example questions:**
- "The author establishes [premise 1] first, then [premise 2]. Why this order? Does premise 2 depend on premise 1?"
- "What is the foundation of this argument? What does everything else rest on?"
- "How is the argument structured: what is the ground floor, and what is built on it?"
- "The author introduces [objection X] before reaching the conclusion. Why raise the objection there? How does it fit into the overall structure?"

### Economics: A Theoretical Framework

**Source:** An explanation of an economic model or theory

**Example questions:**
- "The source first establishes [assumption A]. Why start there? What role does A play?"
- "How does the source build from [basic concept] to [complex concept]? Why that sequence?"
- "What is the foundation of this economic model? What assumptions does it rest on?"
- "The source presents [mechanism X] before discussing [implication Y]. Why? How are they related?"

### History: A Historical Narrative

**Source:** A historical account structured as a chronological or thematic narrative

**Example questions:**
- "The author describes [event A] before [event B]. Why? Does B depend on A?"
- "How is this historical account organized? By chronology, by theme, by geography? Why?"
- "The author spends more pages on [period X] than [period Y]. Why the different emphasis? What is significant about period X?"
- "What is the turning point in this narrative? How does the author structure the account to set it up?"

### Science: A Causal Explanation

**Source:** A scientific explanation of how a mechanism or process works

**Example questions:**
- "The source describes [process 1] before [process 2]. Why this order? Does process 2 depend on process 1?"
- "How is this explanation structured? Does it move from cause to effect, or effect to cause, or something else?"
- "What is foundational in this process? What comes first, and what depends on it?"
- "The source introduces [factor X] at this point in the explanation. Why here? What does introducing it at this moment accomplish?"

## Stage 4: Logical

### Purpose Reminder
Test whether the student can evaluate rigor of reasoning — whether evidence supports claims,
whether inferences are valid, whether there are gaps.

### Law: Evaluating a Court's Inference

**Source:** A court opinion with a claim and supporting reasoning

**Example questions:**
- "The Court concludes [X] based on [evidence Y]. Does Y actually support X? What would Y need to say or establish for it to be stronger?"
- "Is there a gap between the Court's premises and its conclusion? If so, what would need to be true to close it?"
- "The Court assumes [unstated premise A]. Is [A] established in the opinion, or is it just assumed? Where does it come from?"
- "Does the evidence provided adequately support the scope of the conclusion? Is the conclusion narrower or broader than the evidence suggests?"

### Medicine: Evaluating a Study's Inference

**Source:** A study's Results and its stated conclusions

**Example questions:**
- "The study found [result X]. The authors conclude [interpretation Y]. Does X necessarily support Y? What else might explain X?"
- "What assumptions does the study's interpretation rest on? Are those assumptions stated or hidden?"
- "The study's sample was [describe population]. Can the conclusions be applied to [broader or different population]? Why or why not?"
- "Is there a gap between what the data show and what the authors claim? What would need to be true to close it?"

### Philosophy: Evaluating an Argument's Logic

**Source:** A philosophical argument with premises and conclusion

**Example questions:**
- "Does the conclusion follow from the premises? If so, show the steps. If not, what is missing?"
- "What assumption does this argument rest on? Is that assumption true, or is it contested?"
- "The argument uses the term [key concept]. Is that concept defined clearly enough? Would the argument change if the definition were different?"
- "Is this a deductive argument (where the conclusion must follow if the premises are true) or inductive (where the premises make the conclusion likely)? Does it succeed on its own terms?"

### Economics: Evaluating Causal Claims

**Source:** An economic argument claiming that X causes Y

**Example questions:**
- "The source claims [X causes Y]. What evidence does it provide? Is the evidence sufficient to establish causation, or could correlation be explained otherwise?"
- "What assumptions does this causal claim rest on? Are they reasonable?"
- "Could [alternative cause Z] explain the observed effect? How does the source rule it out?"
- "Is the causal mechanism explained? Just that X leads to Y, or is the process described?"

### History: Evaluating Historical Inference

**Source:** A historical account making claims about causes, motivations, or consequences

**Example questions:**
- "The historian claims [consequence C resulted from cause A]. What evidence supports this? Are there alternative explanations?"
- "The source attributes [motivation M] to an actor. Is that attribution based on evidence (e.g., stated intentions, documents) or inference?"
- "Is there a gap between the evidence presented and the claim made? What additional evidence would strengthen the claim?"
- "Could [alternative interpretation] equally fit the evidence? How does the historian argue against it?"

### Science: Evaluating Experimental Logic

**Source:** A research design and the claims it supports

**Example questions:**
- "The experimenters manipulated [variable X] and observed [effect Y]. Does this establish that X causes Y? What confounding variables could explain Y?"
- "What assumption does this experimental design make? (E.g., 'this lab setting represents real-world conditions.')"
- "The study concludes [finding], but the data show [narrower result]. Is the conclusion supported, or overreached?"
- "What is not tested by this experiment? What remains uncertain?"

## Stage 5: Analytical

### Purpose Reminder
Push beyond the text into critical engagement. Test what the source presupposes, what would
need to be true for its reasoning to hold, what it takes for granted.

### Law: Examining Legal Presuppositions

**Source:** A court opinion relying on a legal principle

**Example questions:**
- "This ruling assumes [legal principle P]. Where does P come from? What does P rest on? Is P universally accepted, or contested in legal theory?"
- "The court takes for granted that [unstated legal assumption]. What happens if that assumption is wrong?"
- "What would need to be true about human behavior, institutional capacity, or constitutional power for this rule to work as intended?"
- "This opinion presupposes a certain understanding of [constitutional concept]. What alternative understandings are possible? How would the opinion change under a different understanding?"

### Medicine: Examining Study Presuppositions

**Source:** A clinical study design

**Example questions:**
- "The study design assumes [methodological choice M]. What is that assumption grounded in? What would happen if [M] were false?"
- "What does this study take for granted about the population being studied? How might the study's findings differ in a different population?"
- "The measurement used was [instrument X]. What assumptions does using X rest on? Would [instrument Y] produce the same results?"
- "The study interprets [finding] as evidence of [mechanism]. What alternative mechanisms might produce the same finding?"

### Philosophy: Examining Philosophical Foundations

**Source:** A philosophical argument resting on definitions or concepts

**Example questions:**
- "This argument rests on defining [key concept] as [definition]. What is that definition grounded in? Are alternative definitions possible?"
- "What does the author assume about [fundamental question]? Would the argument work if that assumption were false?"
- "This reasoning takes for granted that [X is true]. Is [X] a necessary truth, a cultural assumption, or something else?"
- "What is the author not examining? What is taken as given without question?"

### Economics: Examining Economic Presuppositions

**Source:** An economic theory or model

**Example questions:**
- "This economic model assumes that humans behave as [rational maximizers / X]. What would happen if people do not behave that way?"
- "The model presupposes [assumption A about markets / information / rationality]. Is [A] realistic? What is it grounded in?"
- "What is this model taking for granted about human nature or social institutions?"
- "The model excludes [factor X] as irrelevant. Why? What happens if [X] matters?"

### History: Examining Historical Presuppositions

**Source:** A historical interpretation

**Example questions:**
- "This interpretation assumes that [social actor X] had [motivation / capability M]. What is that assumption grounded in? Is it reasonable?"
- "The historian takes for granted that [historical condition C] was stable or inevitable. What if [C] were contingent or contested?"
- "What does this interpretation assume about how historical change happens? Is that assumption stated or hidden?"
- "What historical actors' perspectives does this account not include? What difference would their perspective make?"

### Science: Examining Scientific Presuppositions

**Source:** A scientific explanation or model

**Example questions:**
- "This model assumes [simplifying assumption S]. What is [S] grounded in? How does the model change if [S] is relaxed?"
- "The explanation presupposes that [X is the relevant factor]. What other factors might matter? Why did the scientist focus on [X]?"
- "What is the model taking for granted about the system being studied?"
- "The theory assumes that [mechanism M] operates. What is [M] grounded in? Is there evidence for it, or is it posited?"

## Stage 6: Opinion-Seeking

### Purpose Reminder
Ask the student to commit to a view and justify it. This is persuasiveness evaluation, not
preference — the student must ground their opinion in evidence and reasoning.

### Law: Evaluating Legal Persuasiveness

**Source:** A contested legal question with multiple positions in the source or literature

**Example questions:**
- "Do you find the court's reasoning persuasive? Why or why not? What is the strongest objection to it?"
- "Of the positions discussed in the source — [position A] and [position B] — which do you find more compelling?"
- "If you were deciding this case, would you adopt the court's approach? Why? What would you do differently?"
- "What would change your mind about this ruling? What new fact or argument would matter?"

### Medicine: Evaluating Study Persuasiveness

**Source:** A study with limitations or contested implications

**Example questions:**
- "Are you convinced by the authors' interpretation of their findings? Why or why not?"
- "Given the study's limitations, what level of confidence would you place in the findings? What would increase your confidence?"
- "An alternative interpretation of these findings is [X]. Which interpretation — the authors' or [X] — is more persuasive? Why?"
- "If you were recommending a treatment based on this evidence, would you use it? What additional evidence would you want?"

### Philosophy: Evaluating Philosophical Persuasiveness

**Source:** A philosophical argument with objections or alternatives

**Example questions:**
- "Is the author's argument convincing? What is the strongest point? What is the weakest?"
- "The author addresses the objection that [X]. Does the response succeed? Why or why not?"
- "Of the competing positions on [philosophical question] — the author's and [alternative] — which is more persuasive?"
- "What would convince you that the author's view is wrong?"

### Economics: Evaluating Economic Persuasiveness

**Source:** An economic argument or policy recommendation with competing perspectives

**Example questions:**
- "Do you find the economist's argument for [policy X] persuasive? Why or why not?"
- "Given the limitations of economic models, how much weight should we place on [economic claim]?"
- "The economist claims [X will happen]. An alternative view is [Y will happen]. Which is more likely? Why?"
- "What evidence would convince you that this economic analysis is wrong?"

### History: Evaluating Historical Persuasiveness

**Source:** A historical interpretation with alternatives or contested claims

**Example questions:**
- "Do you find this historical interpretation persuasive? What is the strongest evidence for it? The weakest?"
- "An alternative interpretation is [X]. Which interpretation — this one or [X] — is more supported by evidence?"
- "What would change your view of this historical event or period?"
- "If you were writing about this period, what would you emphasize differently than the author?"

### Science: Evaluating Scientific Persuasiveness

**Source:** A scientific claim with supporting evidence and potential limitations

**Example questions:**
- "Are you convinced by the argument that [scientific claim]? Why or why not?"
- "Given the limitations of the study / model / evidence, how confident are you in the conclusion?"
- "A competing explanation for the evidence is [X]. Which explanation — the author's or [X] — is more persuasive?"
- "What additional evidence would be needed to convince you the claim is true?"

## Stage 7: Application

### Purpose Reminder
Test whether understanding is transferable. Can the student apply the framework, principle,
or reasoning from the source to different facts or a novel scenario?

### Law: Applying a Legal Principle

**Source:** A case establishing a legal principle; student applies it to a new fact pattern

**Example questions:**
- "The court established [principle P] in [original case]. If you applied [P] to [new scenario], what would the outcome be? Why?"
- "Would the same reasoning apply if [key fact] were different? How would [P] apply?"
- "The principle from this case is [P]. A [hypothetical scenario] presents a question: does [P] address it? How?"
- "How would this ruling apply to someone who [different fact pattern]?"

### Medicine: Applying a Clinical Principle or Finding

**Source:** A study or clinical guideline; student applies it to a new patient scenario

**Example questions:**
- "The study found [finding] in [population]. If you encountered [different patient with different characteristics], would the finding apply? Why?"
- "The clinical principle from this source is [principle]. A patient presents with [scenario]. How would you apply [principle]?"
- "This treatment was effective for [group]. Would you expect it to work for [different group]? Why or why not?"
- "What would need to be true about a patient for this evidence to apply to them?"

### Philosophy: Applying a Philosophical Framework

**Source:** A philosophical argument or framework; student applies it to a new case

**Example questions:**
- "The author's framework suggests [principle]. If you applied that principle to [different scenario], what would follow?"
- "The author argues that [ethical principle]. Does that principle apply equally to [different case]? Why or why not?"
- "Would this author's reasoning support or oppose [contemporary issue / hypothetical case]?"
- "How would this philosophical framework resolve [a different dilemma]?"

### Economics: Applying an Economic Model

**Source:** An economic principle or model; student applies it to a new scenario

**Example questions:**
- "The source describes how [economic mechanism] operates in [market A]. If you applied that mechanism to [market B], what would you predict?"
- "If [external change X] occurred, how would the economic model explain the new equilibrium?"
- "Would this economic principle apply equally to [different economy / population / time period]?"
- "How would this model predict the impact of [policy change] on [economic outcome]?"

### History: Applying Historical Understanding

**Source:** Historical analysis or pattern; student applies it to a new period or context

**Example questions:**
- "The author's analysis suggests that [historical pattern]. If you encountered [different historical period / context], would the same pattern hold? Why or why not?"
- "What does the author's explanation of [historical process] suggest about [analogous contemporary or different historical process]?"
- "Would the author's interpretation apply to [different event / society / period]? How?"
- "If this historical pattern were true, what would you expect to see in [different context]?"

### Science: Applying a Scientific Principle

**Source:** A scientific principle, mechanism, or finding; student applies it to a new context

**Example questions:**
- "The study established [mechanism] in [system A]. If you applied that mechanism to [system B], what would you predict?"
- "This scientific principle suggests [outcome]. If [new condition] changed, how would the principle apply?"
- "Would the same reasoning explain [analogous phenomenon in a different context]?"
- "If this mechanism is operating in [scenario], what observable evidence would you expect?"

## Progression Notes

### Question Selection Strategy

Do not ask every question above. Use them as examples to generate questions tailored to your
student's specific material and context. Selection strategy:

1. **Start simple:** Begin each stage with a foundational question that tests the core concept
   of that stage.

2. **Assess mastery:** Based on the student's response, determine if they demonstrate understanding
   at that stage. Do not advance until they do.

3. **Choose follow-ups from the same stage:** If the student's response is partial or incomplete,
   ask additional questions from the same stage before advancing.

4. **Adjust to context:** Adapt questions to the student's field, the source material's specific
   content, and their stated purpose for learning.

5. **Watch for regression signals:** If a question at a later stage reveals that earlier
   understanding is shaky, move back to that earlier stage.

### Calibration and Question Phrasing

Adapt question phrasing to Language Level (1-3) and Intensity (1-4):

**Language Level 1 (foundational fluency):**
- Shorter sentences with common vocabulary
- Technical terms explained parenthetically
- One question at a time rather than compound
- More scaffolding in phrasing
- Example: "What is the main idea the author is making? (Not whether you agree — just what is being claimed.)"

**Language Level 2 (working fluency):**
- Standard academic register
- Compound questions where appropriate
- Discipline-appropriate terminology
- Example: "What is the author's central claim, and what reasoning does the author provide to support it?"

**Language Level 3 (advanced fluency):**
- Full academic register
- Dense, multi-layered questions
- Explicit naming of reasoning structures
- Cross-referencing between concepts
- Example: "The author's position on [X] appears to qualify the main argument in section [Y]. How does that qualification modify the overall claim, and does it succeed in addressing potential objections?"

**Intensity 1 (introductory):**
- Narrow scope, foundational questions
- One provocative question per turn
- Scaffolded or multiple-choice format optional
- Example: "What was the study's main finding?"

**Intensity 2 (developing):**
- Two related questions per turn
- Moderate scope
- Example: "What was the study's main finding? What does the study say that finding means?"

**Intensity 3 (rigorous):**
- Three to five related questions per turn
- Deep scope, evaluative questions
- Example: "What was the study's main finding? How does the study interpret that finding? Is that interpretation the only one possible, or could alternative explanations fit the data?"

**Intensity 4 (adversarial):**
- Complex, multi-layered questions
- Requires synthesis across multiple concepts
- Challenges and provocations woven in
- Example: "The study's interpretation assumes that [X]. Yet the data equally fit [alternative explanation Y]. What evidence would the authors need to rule out [Y]? Does the paper address this?"
